# DOTcompass — National Expansion Reconnaissance

> Generated by a 50-state multi-agent reconnaissance workflow (50 Sonnet discovery agents + 50 adversarial verification agents + 1 Opus synthesis), 2026. **This is a scouting report, not ground truth.** The verification pass proved the raw research is unreliable — several source URLs served the wrong state's document, and numbering examples were fabricated for ~17 states. Treat every source below as a *lead to re-derive from the live document*, never as parser input.

## The strategic picture

DOTcompass's Washington pipeline rests on three WSDOT-specific luxuries that most states do NOT share: (1) the hyphenated "1-09.7" division-section token, (2) a fixed 9-division structure, and (3) a full new edition every year with stable section numbers (the sole reason the 26-year history feature works). Nationally: the numbering token generalizes well (most states use a plain three-digit section + dotted decimal subsection, e.g. 105.01), but the annual-edition-with-stable-numbers assumption almost never holds. Roughly 18-20 states publish enough official downloadable editions for a real cross-edition history feature; the remaining ~30 are effectively current-only (1-3 editions, decadal reissues, gated/GRAMA/scan-only archives, or base-edition+supplement overlay models), where DOTcompass degrades to a browser+search+scanner with little or no history. Two structural gotchas dominate the work: many states patch a stable base edition with separate "supplemental specifications" PDFs (so "current text" must be reconstructed, not just downloaded), and the reconnaissance itself is unreliable — several "primary" URLs serve the WRONG state's PDF (Montana and Oklahoma URLs both served Mississippi's 2017 book; the Texas URL served New Mexico's), and many numbering EXAMPLES in the recon are fabricated or mis-titled (verified false for AL, CO, FL, NC, SD, WI, VA). Every source and every TOC structure must be re-derived from the actual live document before ingesting; do not trust the recon's example citations.

## Numbering-scheme clusters

One parser can serve a whole cluster.

- **WSDOT hyphenated division-section-subsection (D-SS.n, e.g. 1-09.7)** — 1 states: Washington
- **AASHTO hundreds-division / three-digit section / dotted-decimal subsection (Section 105 -> 105.01 or 105.1, nesting like 105.15.2.1)** — 36 states: Alabama, Arizona, Colorado, Delaware, Idaho, Illinois, Indiana, Kentucky, Michigan, Mississippi, Missouri, Montana, Nebraska, New Jersey, New Mexico, North Dakota, Oklahoma, Maryland, Maine, Georgia, Nevada, New Hampshire, Pennsylvania, South Carolina, Tennessee, Wyoming, Virginia, Ohio, Florida, Kansas, Rhode Island, Vermont, West Virginia, Hawaii, Arkansas, Wisconsin
- **Hyphen-subsection variant (NNN-n or SSS-n.nn, e.g. 105-1 / 203-3.02)** — 2 states: North Carolina, New York
- **Roman-numeral Part/Division container with non-hundreds or prefixed section numbering** — 4 states: Louisiana, Connecticut, South Dakota, Massachusetts
- **Four-digit self-encoding section numbers (SectionNNNN.sub, e.g. 2102.01 / 2105.501)** — 2 states: Iowa, Minnesota
- **Five-digit CSI-style / Item-number schemes** — 3 states: Oregon, Utah, Texas

## Tractability tiers

### tier1 (19 states)
Small generalization of the WSDOT parser: clean single born-digital PDF, cluster-2 dotted-decimal numbering, directly fetchable without bot-blocking or JS/Drive tricks. Text-layer extraction confirmed clean during recon for most. Primary work is generalizing the subsection tokenizer and driving structure from each state's TOC.

Pennsylvania, Missouri, Indiana, North Dakota, Idaho, Delaware, Maryland, Wyoming, Nebraska, Mississippi, Maine, South Carolina, Tennessee, Rhode Island, Virginia, West Virginia, Ohio, Colorado, Florida

### tier2 (20 states)
Different scheme and/or packaging but clean born-digital PDFs; needs a new-but-straightforward parser or a multi-file assembler, or a base+supplement overlay model. Includes hyphen-subsection (NC/NY), 4-digit (IA/MN), 5-digit/Item (OR/TX), Roman-part (LA/CT/SD), per-division/zip packaging (AR/WI/KY/MA), and stable-base-plus-supplement reconstruction (IL/AZ/GA). Fetch is workable but not trivial.

North Carolina, New York, Iowa, Minnesota, Oregon, Texas, Louisiana, Connecticut, South Dakota, Arkansas, Wisconsin, Kentucky, Illinois, Arizona, Georgia, Alabama, Oklahoma, Montana, New Mexico, Nevada

### tier3 (9 states)
Genuinely painful: automated fetch blocked (403 bot-walls), non-stable/expiring hosting, JS-widget or API-gated file lists, per-section fragmentation, records-request-only history, or OCR required for historical scans. Several are current-only regardless of effort. New Mexico's RealFile JS/AWS-API widget and Utah's Google-Drive-folders-plus-GRAMA are the worst ingestion targets; Massachusetts/Michigan/New Jersey/Kansas/New Hampshire/Nevada return 403 to scrapers and need headless-browser fetch; Hawaii is ~95 per-section revision-lettered PDFs; older editions for Louisiana (pre-2006), Arizona (pre-2021), North Carolina (2002-2012), Minnesota (pre-2005), and Tennessee (pre-2015) exist only as library scans requiring OCR.

Utah, New Mexico, Hawaii, Massachusetts, Michigan, New Jersey, Kansas, New Hampshire, Alaska

## Does the section-history feature even work?

Only about 18-20 states publish enough official, downloadable, multi-edition history for DOTcompass's flagship cross-edition section-history feature to actually function — and even there, section numbers are NOT guaranteed stable across editions the way WSDOT's are, so a fuzzy section-alignment/matching layer is required (do not assume same-number = same-section). GENUINELY DEEP official archives (feature works well): Washington (2000-2026), Florida (~25 yrs semi-annual back to 2000, PLUS a per-section 'History of Revisions' index — best-in-class), Pennsylvania (2000-2026, 7 editions + change packets), Wisconsin (1996-2025, 20+ editions, though per-part zip and numbering caveats), Iowa (2010-present semiannual ERL snapshots, section-addressable HTML — arguably better than WSDOT for diffing), Missouri (annual 2016-2026), Indiana (2008-2026 biennial + redline docs), Maryland (annual 2008-2026), North Dakota (annual 2020-2025), Delaware (2001-2026), Kentucky (2004-2026, 5 editions per-division), Virginia (1997-2020 + ContentDM older), Minnesota (2005-2025 via digital library), New York (2012-present, 3x/year), Rhode Island (2018-2026, ~15 six-month reprints), Louisiana (1929-2026, ~13 editions but pre-2006 need OCR), Vermont (1995-2024 digital + 1948), Alabama (2012/2018/2022/2026). MODERATE/DECADAL (history exists but thin, 3-5 editions, big gaps, or change-summary-only): Ohio, Idaho, Oklahoma, Illinois, Arizona, North Carolina, Maine, New Mexico, South Carolina, Texas, Georgia, Tennessee, Connecticut, Montana (edition-versioned). EFFECTIVELY CURRENT-ONLY (no usable history — browser+search+scanner only): Nebraska (2017 only), Kansas (2015 only), Michigan (2012+2020, flagged historicalEditionsAvailable=false), Nevada (2001+2014), Wyoming (2010+2021), South Dakota (2015+2025), New Hampshire (2010+2016), Colorado (only 2 free; older gated behind email request), Alaska (2-3 editions, none official older than 2015), Arkansas (2003+2014), Mississippi (2004+2017), Hawaii (1994+2005), Utah (2-3 rotating editions, older only via GRAMA records request). SEPARATE HARD MODE — OVERLAY STATES: New Jersey (2019 base + 80+ BDC patch PDFs), West Virginia (2023 base + annual supplements), Illinois/Arizona/Idaho/Georgia/Massachusetts (stable base + separate supplemental specs): editions look sparse but 'current text' and change-history live in the amendment stream, requiring a different data model than 'one PDF per year.'

## Recommended pilot states

- **Pennsylvania** — Best overall value/effort. Clean single 1,522-page born-digital PDF fetched cleanly in recon; cluster-2 dotted-decimal numbering (103.07) verified exactly right; deep official archive 2000-2026 (7 editions) PLUS per-edition numbered 'Change' packets for fine-grained history. Both browse+search AND the flagship history feature work with a straightforward parser generalization.
- **Missouri** — Annual editions 2016-2026 (~11 clean data points, closest cadence to WSDOT's annual model), single certified PDF verified live, dotted-decimal with deep nesting (105.15.2.1) verified. Ideal for exercising cross-edition alignment on a rich, stable, low-friction source.
- **Indiana** — 10 biennial editions 2008-2026 at predictable per-edition URLs, single combined PDF verified, numbering (105.03) verified exactly, and INDOT publishes redline 'list of revisions' diffs per edition — directly useful for validating the history-diff engine. (Recon's 'per-division-pdf' format tag is wrong; it is one combined book.)
- **North Dakota** — Five annual editions 2020-2025 all on the DOT domain, single PDF verified live with clean pdfplumber extraction, numbering (105.01/106.02) verified exactly. Low-risk, fast win that proves the generalized parser plus a short history stack.
- **Florida** — Highest-value history target: ~25 years of semi-annual editions back to 2000 and a unique per-section 'History of Revisions' index that maps almost perfectly onto DOTcompass's feature, single PDF, no login. Caveat to design around: FDOT uses only 3 Roman-numeral divisions and Section 105 is NOT 'Control of Materials' (recon's example is wrong), so derive structure from the live TOC — a good forcing function for making the parser TOC-driven rather than hard-coded.

## Phased plan

### Phase 0 — Source hardening and re-verification (prerequisite)
**Goal:** Stop trusting the reconnaissance; establish a reliable, auditable source layer.

Build a per-state source registry and a robust fetcher that handles headless-browser rendering (for 403 bot-walls: MA/MI/NJ/KS/NH/NV), Google Drive folders (UT), and JS/AWS-API widgets (NM RealFile). Re-fetch EVERY primary URL and assert the document is the claimed state's current edition — recon proved multiple URLs serve the wrong state (MT and OK served Mississippi; TX served New Mexico). Record file hash, page count, edition label, and a TOC fingerprint per source. Flag copyright/reuse per state (several assert All Rights Reserved).

### Phase 1 — Generalize the parser; ship current-edition browse+search for pilots
**Goal:** Turn the WSDOT-specific parser into a TOC-driven, configurable AASHTO parser and validate on the easiest high-value states.

Replace the hard-coded 1-09.7 tokenizer and fixed 9-division assumption with a per-state config: subsection regex, division-to-topic map, and nesting depth all derived from the live TOC. Ship browse + full-text search + current-PDF scanner for the 5 pilots (PA, MO, IN, ND, FL). Explicitly do NOT assume Section 105 titles/structure from recon notes.

### Phase 2 — History engine on the deep, clean archives
**Goal:** Prove the flagship cross-edition feature where it genuinely works, with a real alignment layer.

Enumerate year-stamped editions for PA, MO, IN, ND, FL, then extend to MD, KY, DE, VA. Build a fuzzy section-alignment matcher (title + content similarity, not just number equality) because most states renumber across editions unlike WSDOT. Validate against Indiana/Florida redline/revision-index docs as ground truth. Add the base-edition + supplement overlay model here for FL-style semi-annual and for overlay states.

### Phase 3 — Breadth across the big AASHTO single-PDF cluster
**Goal:** Cover the majority of states at current-edition depth, adding history only where archives exist.

Roll the generalized parser across the remaining tier-1 dotted-decimal states (WY, NE, MS, ME, SC, TN, RI, WV, OH, CO, plus re-sourced AL/OK/MT/NV/NM/AZ/GA). Add headless fetch for the 403-blocked members. Turn on history for the moderate-archive members (OH, ID, IL, AZ, TN, CT); leave current-only states (KS, MI, SD, NH, AK, AR) as browse+search+scan with history explicitly disabled in the UI.

### Phase 4 — New parsers for divergent clean-PDF schemes
**Goal:** Cover the states whose numbering breaks the cluster-2 tokenizer but whose PDFs are clean.

Build the hyphen-subsection variant (NC, NY — NY also multi-volume), the 4-digit self-encoding parser (IA, MN — and wire up Iowa's HTML ERL as a first-class deep-history source), the 5-digit/Item parsers (OR, UT-CSI, TX-Item), and the Roman-part container parser (LA, CT, SD, MA). Add per-division/zip assembly for AR, WI, KY, MA.

### Phase 5 — Long tail and OCR
**Goal:** Best-effort coverage of the painful sources; accept many as current-only.

Utah (Google Drive snapshotting before folders rotate + GRAMA for back-editions), Hawaii (per-section revision-letter assembly), and OCR pipelines for historical scans (LA pre-2006, AZ pre-2021 azmemory, NC 2002-2012 ContentDM, MN pre-2005, TN pre-2015 Memphis archive) with quality gating. Formalize the amendment-overlay reconstruction for NJ (BDCs), WV, IL, AZ, GA, MA where 'current text' is not a single file.

## Risks — stated plainly

1) THE HISTORY FEATURE MOSTLY DOES NOT EXIST ELSEWHERE. WSDOT's annual-full-edition-with-stable-numbers practice is a rare luxury. Only ~18-20 states have usable multi-edition official archives; ~15+ are current-only or two-edition, and several gate history behind email/GRAMA records requests (CO, UT) or third-party library scans. Marketing DOTcompass as a national 'section history' tool would be dishonest — for most states it is a browser+search+scanner. 2) SECTION NUMBERS ARE NOT STABLE ACROSS EDITIONS in most states, so even where multiple editions exist, naive number-matching will mis-align sections. A content/title-similarity alignment layer is mandatory and will sometimes be wrong. 3) BASE-EDITION + SUPPLEMENT OVERLAY MODEL. IDOT, WVDOT, NJDOT (80+ BDCs), Idaho, Arizona (Stored Specs), Georgia, Massachusetts (SPIs) keep one old base book and patch it with separate amendment PDFs. 'Current text' must be reconstructed by layering amendments — a fundamentally different data model than 'download one PDF,' and the hardest part of the change-history feature for these states. 4) RECON DATA IS UNRELIABLE. Multiple 'primary' URLs served the wrong state's document (MT->MS, OK->MS, TX->NM), and numbering EXAMPLES were fabricated or mis-titled in many records that passed URL checks (verified-false example/structure for AL, CO, FL, NC, SD, WI, VA — e.g. Virginia's division titles are largely wrong, NC's division mapping is scrambled, Wisconsin's '105.03' does not exist). Any parser built from these notes rather than the live TOC will inherit the errors; every structure must be re-derived from the actual document. 5) ACCESS FRICTION defeats a static crawler. mass.gov, michigan.gov, ksdot, nh, nj, nevada, ardot return 403 to scrapers (need headless browser); Utah distributes via expiring Google Drive links; New Mexico requires replicating a JS/AWS-API widget. Ingestion is a moving target that will break silently. 6) COPYRIGHT/REUSE IS OFTEN RESTRICTIVE OR UNSTATED. Arizona, Indiana, New Jersey, Wisconsin, South Carolina, South Dakota, Wyoming, Michigan, and Rhode Island assert 'All Rights Reserved'; Wyoming's related standards use click-through terms forbidding redistribution. Most others are silent (legally ambiguous, not confirmed public-domain). Hosting/redistributing full spec text — and especially scanned reprints — carries real legal risk that must be cleared per state before ingestion, not assumed. 7) OCR QUALITY for the deepest historical value (Louisiana's 1929-1990s, Arizona/NC/MN older editions) will introduce section-boundary and text errors that degrade exactly the history feature that motivated those sources."
